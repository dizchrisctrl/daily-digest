⚠ Vulnerability Advisory

22 "BRIDGE:BREAK" Vulnerabilities Put 20,000 Serial-to-IP Converters at Risk of Hijacking

Critical ⚠ Under Investigation

Forescout Vedere Labs discovered 22 vulnerabilities collectively named "BRIDGE:BREAK" affecting Lantronix and Silex serial-to-IP converters, with nearly 20,000 internet-facing devices at risk. The flaws — including authentication bypasses, command injection, and buffer overflows — could allow attackers to hijack converters, intercept or manipulate serial data flowing between legacy OT equipment and IP networks, and pivot into isolated industrial and healthcare environments.

📅 Apr 21, 2026 11:46 AM EDT| Source: The Hacker News ↗
▶Attack Flow
BRIDGE:BREAK — 22 Vulnerabilities in Lantronix and Silex Serial-to-IP Converters
Attackers can exploit a range of flaws (authentication bypasses, command injection, buffer overflows, insecure defaults) in internet-exposed serial-to-IP converters to gain unauthorized access, execute arbitrary commands, and establish a man-in-the-middle position on serial data flowing between legacy OT devices and modern IP networks.

❓Am I Affected?

You are likely at risk if any of the following apply:

⚠You have Lantronix or Silex serial-to-IP converters deployed in your environment
⚠Your serial-to-IP converters are exposed to the internet or accessible from untrusted network segments
⚠Your environment uses serial-to-IP converters to bridge legacy medical devices, industrial controllers, or building automation systems to IP networks
⚠Your serial device servers still use factory-default credentials or have not had firmware updated in over 12 months
✓You do not use any serial-to-IP converters or serial device servers in your environment
✓Your serial-to-IP converters are fully isolated on a dedicated network segment with no internet exposure and strict access controls
🛠Fix & Recommendations
⚠ Immediate 24–72 hours
  • 1Conduct an immediate asset inventory to identify all Lantronix and Silex serial-to-IP converters across IT, OT, and healthcare network segments — check Shodan/Censys for any internet-exposed instances belonging to your organization.
  • 2Remove all affected converters from direct internet exposure immediately by placing them behind firewalls or VPN gateways; block inbound access to management ports (typically TCP 80, 443, 9999 for Lantronix; TCP 80, 443 for Silex).
  • 3Change all default and hardcoded credentials on converter management interfaces; disable any unused management protocols (Telnet, HTTP) and enable HTTPS where supported.
  • 4Apply network segmentation to isolate serial-to-IP converters into a dedicated VLAN with strict ACLs permitting only authorized hosts to communicate with the converter management and data ports.
  • 5Monitor vendor advisories from Lantronix and Silex for firmware patches addressing the BRIDGE:BREAK vulnerabilities, and subscribe to Forescout's disclosure updates for specific CVE assignments.
● Strategic This week & beyond
  • 1Implement a comprehensive OT/IoT asset management program that includes embedded bridge devices like serial-to-IP converters in regular vulnerability scanning and firmware lifecycle management.
  • 2Deploy network detection and response (NDR) or OT-aware intrusion detection systems capable of monitoring serial-over-IP traffic for anomalous patterns, unauthorized configuration changes, or data manipulation.
  • 3Adopt a zero-trust segmentation architecture for IT/OT boundary devices — require authenticated, encrypted tunnels (e.g., IPsec, WireGuard) for any traffic traversing serial-to-IP converters rather than relying on implicit trust.
  • 4Establish a replacement roadmap for end-of-life serial-to-IP converters that cannot receive security updates, prioritizing devices in safety-critical environments (healthcare, industrial control systems).
  • 5Integrate serial-to-IP converter security into procurement and deployment standards, requiring devices with signed firmware updates, no hardcoded credentials, and encrypted management interfaces before approval.
📚Concept BriefingClick a tag to expand
Serial-to-IP Converter
A small embedded device that translates legacy serial communication (RS-232/RS-485) into TCP/IP network packets, allowing old equipment to communicate over modern networks.
These converters are the core devices affected by BRIDGE:BREAK — they sit inline between legacy OT equipment and IP networks, making them high-value targets for interception and manipulation.
IT/OT Convergence
The trend of connecting traditionally isolated operational technology (OT) systems like industrial controllers and medical devices to standard IT networks and the internet.
Serial-to-IP converters are the physical embodiment of IT/OT convergence, and their compromise bridges the gap attackers need to reach from internet-facing networks into critical operational systems.
Authentication Bypass
A vulnerability that allows an attacker to gain access to a system without providing valid credentials, circumventing login or access control mechanisms entirely.
Authentication bypass is one of the primary vulnerability classes in BRIDGE:BREAK, enabling unauthenticated remote attackers to take control of converter management interfaces.
Command Injection
A flaw that allows an attacker to insert and execute arbitrary operating system commands through an application's input fields or parameters.
Command injection vulnerabilities in the web management interfaces of affected converters allow attackers to execute arbitrary code on the embedded device after gaining access.
Embedded Device Security
The practice of securing small, purpose-built computing devices that run minimal operating systems and are often deployed in set-and-forget configurations with no regular patching.
The BRIDGE:BREAK vulnerabilities highlight the systemic risk of embedded devices that lack automatic updates, use hardcoded credentials, and were designed for isolated networks now connected to the internet.
🔍Threat Hunting Signals
High Unexpected inbound connections to serial-to-IP converter management ports ►
Look for external or unauthorized internal IP addresses connecting to Lantronix management ports (TCP 9999, 80, 443) or Silex management ports (TCP 80, 443). Any connection from outside the authorized management subnet may indicate reconnaissance or exploitation attempts against BRIDGE:BREAK flaws.
Firewall logsNetFlow/sFlow recordsIDS/IPS alertsNetwork switch port mirroring
High Authentication failures or anomalous logins on converter web interfaces ►
Monitor web server logs or SIEM alerts for brute-force login attempts, successful logins using default credentials (e.g., 'admin/admin', 'sysadmin'), or authentication bypass patterns such as direct access to administrative endpoints without prior login on converter management interfaces.
Device web interface logs (if available)Reverse proxy logsSIEM correlation rules
High Configuration changes or firmware modifications on serial device servers ►
Detect unauthorized changes to converter settings such as serial port parameters, network configuration, or firmware version. Compare current device configurations against known-good baselines. Unexpected changes may indicate an attacker has gained control of the device.
OT asset management platformsConfiguration backup/diff toolsSNMP trap logs
Medium Unusual outbound traffic from serial-to-IP converter IP addresses ►
Serial-to-IP converters should have predictable, limited traffic patterns. Look for outbound connections to external IP addresses, DNS lookups to unknown domains, or traffic to internal hosts outside normal serial data destinations — which may indicate a compromised converter being used for pivoting or data exfiltration.
Firewall logsNetFlow/sFlow recordsDNS query logsNDR platforms
Medium Anomalous serial-over-IP data patterns or protocol violations ►
If you capture serial-over-IP traffic (e.g., Lantronix tunneling protocol, RFC 2217), look for unexpected changes in data volume, timing, or content compared to baseline serial communication patterns. Data manipulation by an attacker at the converter level may manifest as altered sensor values or injected commands.
