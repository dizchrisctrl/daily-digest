⚠ Vulnerability Advisory

20 Vulnerabilities in Serial-to-IP Converters Threaten OT and Healthcare Infrastructure

Critical ⚠ Under Investigation

Forescout's Vedere Labs disclosed 20 new vulnerabilities across Lantronix and Silex serial-to-IP converters — small embedded devices that bridge legacy serial equipment (medical instruments, industrial controllers, utility systems) to modern IP networks. These flaws could theoretically allow attackers to pivot from IT networks through the converters into serial-connected OT and healthcare devices, manipulating commands or intercepting sensitive data. The findings highlight a systemic risk across tens of thousands of deployed devices that are rarely inventoried, monitored, or patched.

📅 Apr 20, 2026 11:42 AM EDT| Source: SecurityWeek ↗
▶Attack Flow
20 Vulnerabilities in Lantronix and Silex Serial-to-IP Converters Threatening OT and Healthcare Infrastructure
Attackers can exploit vulnerabilities in the web management interfaces, Telnet services, or SNMP configurations of serial-to-IP converters — many of which run default credentials and unencrypted protocols — to gain control of the converter and then pivot through the serial connection to manipulate or intercept commands to downstream OT and medical devices.

❓Am I Affected?

You are likely at risk if any of the following apply:

⚠You use Lantronix or Silex serial-to-IP converters in your environment
⚠Your environment includes legacy serial devices (medical instruments, PLCs, RTUs) connected to IP networks via any serial device server
⚠Your serial-to-IP converters are accessible from general-purpose IT network segments without segmentation
⚠You have serial-to-IP converters running with default or factory credentials
✓Your serial-to-IP converters are deployed on an isolated, air-gapped network segment with no IP routing to corporate or external networks
✓You do not use any serial-to-IP converters or serial device servers in your environment
🛠Fix & Recommendations
⚠ Immediate 24–72 hours
  • 1Conduct an immediate asset inventory scan to identify all Lantronix and Silex serial-to-IP converters on your network — check ARP tables, MAC OUI lookups (Lantronix OUI 00:20:4A, Silex OUI 00:80:92), and OT asset discovery tools.
  • 2Change all default credentials on identified converters immediately; disable Telnet access and enable SSH or HTTPS where supported by the device firmware.
  • 3Place all serial-to-IP converters behind a firewall or VLAN with strict ACLs that permit only necessary management and data traffic from authorized hosts — block all inbound access from general IT segments and the internet.
  • 4Disable unused network services on converters (SNMP v1/v2, HTTP, Telnet) via the device configuration interface to reduce attack surface.
  • 5Monitor vendor advisories from Lantronix and Silex for forthcoming patches and CVE assignments related to these 20 vulnerabilities.
● Strategic This week & beyond
  • 1Implement network microsegmentation that isolates all serial-to-IP converters and their connected OT/medical devices into dedicated zones with monitored, policy-enforced communication paths (Purdue Model Level 1-2 segmentation).
  • 2Deploy an OT-aware network monitoring solution (e.g., Forescout, Claroty, Nozomi Networks) that can passively fingerprint serial-to-IP converters and detect anomalous traffic patterns crossing the IT/OT boundary.
  • 3Establish a lifecycle management program for embedded OT devices — include serial-to-IP converters in vulnerability scanning, asset inventory, and firmware patching workflows alongside other network infrastructure.
  • 4Evaluate replacing end-of-life or fundamentally unpatchable serial-to-IP converters with modern alternatives that support encrypted transport, certificate-based authentication, and centralized management.
  • 5Conduct tabletop exercises simulating a converter compromise to validate incident response procedures for OT and healthcare environments where these devices are deployed.
📚Concept BriefingClick a tag to expand
Serial-to-IP Converter
A small embedded device that translates legacy serial communication (RS-232, RS-485) into TCP/IP packets, allowing old equipment to connect to modern networks.
These converters are the specific device class affected by all 20 discovered vulnerabilities, and they bridge the critical trust boundary between IT and OT/medical networks.
IT/OT Convergence
The process of connecting traditionally isolated operational technology networks (factories, utilities, hospitals) to enterprise IT networks and the internet.
Serial-to-IP converters are a primary mechanism enabling IT/OT convergence, and their vulnerabilities expose the security risks inherent in bridging these previously separate environments.
Air Gap
A security measure where a network or device is physically isolated from other networks, especially the internet, to prevent remote access.
Serial-to-IP converters effectively eliminate the air gap that once protected legacy serial devices, creating a network-accessible attack path to equipment that was never designed to be remotely reachable.
Default Credentials
Factory-set usernames and passwords that ship with devices and are often never changed by administrators after deployment.
Many serial-to-IP converters run with default credentials, providing trivial initial access for attackers without requiring exploitation of any software vulnerability.
Shadow OT Assets
Operational technology devices deployed in an environment that do not appear in asset inventories or vulnerability management systems.
Serial-to-IP converters are rarely tracked in asset inventories, meaning organizations often don't know how many they have, where they are, or what they connect — making them invisible attack surfaces.
🔍Threat Hunting Signals
High Unexpected network connections to serial-to-IP converter management interfaces ►
Look for HTTP, HTTPS, Telnet (TCP/23), or SSH connections to known serial-to-IP converter IP addresses from hosts that are not authorized management stations. Unusual source IPs or access outside business hours may indicate reconnaissance or exploitation attempts.
Firewall logsNetwork flow data (NetFlow/sFlow)VLAN/switch access logs
High Authentication failures or default credential usage on converter web interfaces ►
Monitor for repeated authentication failures or successful logins using known default credentials (e.g., admin/admin, admin/PASS, sysadmin/PASS) against Lantronix or Silex device management interfaces. These devices typically log minimally, so network-based detection via HTTP request inspection may be necessary.
Web proxy logsIDS/IPS alertsNetwork packet captures
Medium SNMP enumeration targeting converter OIDs ►
Detect SNMP GET/WALK requests targeting Lantronix or Silex-specific MIB OIDs, especially using default community strings ('public', 'private'). This often precedes exploitation as attackers enumerate device configuration and firmware versions.
IDS/IPS logsNetwork flow dataSNMP trap receivers
Medium Anomalous serial port traffic volume or patterns through converters ►
If you have network visibility into converter traffic, look for sudden changes in data volume, packet frequency, or new TCP connections on serial-to-IP data ports (commonly TCP 10001, 9100, or vendor-specific ports) that deviate from established baselines for connected serial devices.
OT network monitoring toolsNetwork flow dataPacket captures on converter segments
High Firmware update or configuration change attempts on converters ►
Watch for TFTP, FTP, or HTTP POST requests to converter IP addresses that could indicate firmware replacement or configuration tampering. Unauthorized firmware changes could introduce backdoors or disable security controls.
