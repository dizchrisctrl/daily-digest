FBI and Indonesian Police Take Down W3LL Phishing Empire Behind $20M in Fraud

High ⚠ Mitigation Only

A joint FBI and Indonesian National Police operation dismantled the W3LL phishing-as-a-service (PhaaS) platform, arrested its alleged developer, and disrupted an operation responsible for stealing thousands of credentials and enabling over $20 million in attempted fraud. W3LL provided turnkey phishing kits with adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) capabilities that allowed buyers to bypass MFA and harvest session tokens at scale. The takedown targets the supply side of the phishing ecosystem, but the commoditized model it popularized will likely persist as other operators emerge.

📅 Apr 13, 2026 10:46 AM EDT| Source: The Hacker News ↗
▶Attack Flow
W3LL Phishing-as-a-Service Empire Takedown by FBI and Indonesian Police
W3LL provided pre-built phishing pages mimicking services like Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace, equipped with adversary-in-the-middle reverse proxies that intercepted session tokens and MFA codes in real time. Buyers used the platform's email delivery tools and anti-detection features to launch credential harvesting campaigns at scale, with stolen credentials then leveraged for business email compromise and financial fraud.

❓Am I Affected?

You are likely at risk if any of the following apply:

⚠Your organization uses Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace with SMS, push, or TOTP-based MFA
⚠Your users regularly receive external emails containing links to login pages
⚠Your organization lacks conditional access policies that enforce managed device or compliant network requirements for authentication
⚠Your email security gateway does not inspect URLs for AiTM proxy indicators or newly registered domains
✓You have already deployed FIDO2 security keys or passkeys as your primary MFA method and disabled fallback to phishable factors
✓Your organization enforces token binding or continuous access evaluation policies that invalidate sessions from unrecognized devices or locations
🛠Fix & Recommendations
⚠ Immediate 24–72 hours
  • 1Audit Azure AD / Entra ID and Google Workspace sign-in logs for suspicious session anomalies: look for sign-ins where the MFA claim was satisfied but the device, IP, or location is unrecognized (filter on riskState, riskLevel, and unusual user-agent strings).
  • 2Enable Continuous Access Evaluation (CAE) in Microsoft Entra ID or Google's context-aware access to detect and revoke tokens used from unexpected networks or devices.
  • 3Review and tighten conditional access policies to require compliant/managed devices for accessing sensitive resources — block legacy authentication protocols entirely.
  • 4Alert your SOC to increase monitoring for inbound emails containing URLs with redirects through newly registered domains or known AiTM proxy patterns (e.g., Cloudflare Workers, reverse proxy infrastructure on commodity VPS providers).
  • 5Communicate to all staff an immediate advisory about the W3LL takedown, reinforcing that even completing a 'normal-looking' MFA prompt on a phishing page can result in full account compromise.
● Strategic This week & beyond
  • 1Migrate to phishing-resistant MFA organization-wide: deploy FIDO2 security keys or platform passkeys and disable fallback to SMS, TOTP, or push-based MFA for all accounts, starting with privileged and finance roles.
  • 2Implement token protection / token binding policies (Microsoft's Token Protection in Entra ID or equivalent) to cryptographically bind session tokens to the device that performed authentication, rendering stolen tokens unusable on attacker infrastructure.
  • 3Deploy a Zero Trust architecture that evaluates device posture, network location, and real-time risk signals at every access request — not just at initial authentication.
  • 4Invest in email security solutions with real-time URL sandboxing and AiTM detection capabilities that can identify reverse-proxy phishing pages even when they serve legitimate content.
  • 5Conduct regular red-team exercises that specifically simulate AiTM phishing attacks to test detection and response capabilities, and use results to refine conditional access and SOC playbooks.
📚Concept BriefingClick a tag to expand
Phishing-as-a-Service
A criminal business model where developers sell ready-made phishing toolkits — including fake login pages, email delivery tools, and admin panels — as subscription services to other cybercriminals.
W3LL was a leading PhaaS platform whose takedown disrupts one supplier but highlights how the commoditization of phishing tools lowers the barrier for credential theft globally.
Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM)
An attack technique where a reverse proxy sits between the victim and a legitimate login service, relaying the real login page while secretly capturing credentials and session tokens in transit.
W3LL's AiTM capabilities were its key differentiator, allowing buyers to bypass standard MFA by stealing authenticated session cookies after victims completed genuine login flows.
Phishing-Resistant MFA
Authentication methods like FIDO2 security keys and passkeys that cryptographically bind the login to the legitimate domain, making them immune to interception by phishing pages or proxy attacks.
Organizations using phishing-resistant MFA would have been protected against W3LL's AiTM attacks because the credential cannot be replayed from a fake or proxy domain.
Session Token Theft
The act of stealing an already-authenticated session cookie or token, allowing an attacker to impersonate a logged-in user without needing their password or MFA code again.
W3LL's toolkit captured session tokens post-authentication, meaning even users who correctly completed MFA had their accounts compromised through stolen session cookies.
Supply-Side Disruption
A law enforcement strategy that targets the developers, infrastructure providers, and sellers of cybercrime tools rather than pursuing individual attackers who use them.
The FBI and Indonesian police focused on dismantling W3LL's infrastructure and arresting its developer — attacking the supply chain that enabled thousands of downstream phishing campaigns.
🔍Threat Hunting Signals
High AiTM session token replay from anomalous infrastructure ►
Look for sign-in events where a user authenticates from one IP/location and then, within minutes, the same session token is used from a different IP — particularly from VPS providers, hosting services, or known proxy infrastructure. In Entra ID, filter sign-in logs for 'Anomalous Token' risk detections or events where riskEventTypes include 'unfamiliarFeatures' shortly after MFA satisfaction.
Azure AD / Entra ID Sign-in LogsGoogle Workspace Login Audit LogsCASB Session Logs
High Inbox rule manipulation post-compromise ►
After credential theft via AiTM, attackers commonly create mail forwarding rules or inbox rules to hide BEC activity. Search for new inbox rules that forward or delete emails containing keywords like 'invoice', 'payment', 'wire', or 'bank'. In Exchange Online, use Get-InboxRule or Unified Audit Log filtering on 'New-InboxRule' and 'Set-InboxRule' operations.
Microsoft 365 Unified Audit LogExchange Online PowerShell (Get-InboxRule)Google Workspace Admin Audit Logs
High Phishing emails with AiTM proxy URL patterns ►
Monitor inbound email for URLs that redirect through newly registered domains (< 30 days old), use subdomains mimicking Microsoft or Google login pages, or resolve to known reverse-proxy infrastructure. Look for HTML attachments containing obfuscated JavaScript that redirects to external authentication proxies.
Email Gateway Logs (Proofpoint, Mimecast, Defender for Office 365)DNS Query LogsWeb Proxy Logs
Medium OAuth application consent or app registration anomalies ►
After stealing session tokens, attackers may register malicious OAuth apps or consent to third-party apps for persistent access. Hunt for unusual OAuth consent grants, new app registrations by non-admin users, or apps requesting Mail.Read, Mail.Send, or Files.ReadWrite.All permissions.
Azure AD / Entra ID Audit LogsGoogle Workspace OAuth Token Audit LogsMicrosoft Cloud App Security Alerts
Medium Bulk credential validation from unusual sources ►
W3LL operators or their customers may test stolen credentials in bulk. Look for multiple failed or successful authentications for different user accounts originating from the same IP block within a short time window, especially from non-corporate IP ranges.
