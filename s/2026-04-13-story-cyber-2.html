FBI and Indonesian Police Dismantle W3LL Phishing Empire Behind $20M in Fraud

High ⚠ Mitigation Only

A joint FBI and Indonesian National Police operation has dismantled the W3LL phishing-as-a-service infrastructure and detained its alleged developer. W3LL was a subscription-based phishing toolkit that enabled thousands of cybercriminals worldwide to steal credentials at scale — including bypassing multi-factor authentication via adversary-in-the-middle session hijacking — fueling over $20 million in attempted fraud. This takedown represents a strategic law enforcement shift toward disrupting the cybercrime supply chain at its source.

📅 Apr 13, 2026 10:46 AM EDT| Source: The Hacker News ↗
▶Attack Flow
W3LL Phishing-as-a-Service Empire Dismantled by FBI and Indonesian Police
W3LL operated as a phishing-as-a-service platform providing subscribers with ready-made phishing page templates (mimicking Microsoft 365, Google Workspace, and other login portals), credential harvesting backends, and adversary-in-the-middle (AiTM) proxy capabilities that intercepted session cookies in real time to bypass MFA. Customers distributed phishing links via email, SMS, or social media, and harvested credentials were used for downstream fraud operations.

❓Am I Affected?

You are likely at risk if any of the following apply:

⚠Your organization uses Microsoft 365 or Google Workspace with SMS, TOTP, or push-based MFA (not FIDO2/passkeys)
⚠Your users routinely receive external emails and could encounter phishing links
⚠Your organization lacks conditional access policies that evaluate device compliance, location, or sign-in risk before granting access
✓Your environment has already deployed FIDO2/passkey-based phishing-resistant MFA for all users and disabled legacy authentication
⚠Your security team does not monitor for anomalous session token usage or impossible-travel sign-in events
✓Your organization provides regular, updated phishing awareness training that covers AiTM proxy attacks
🛠Fix & Recommendations
⚠ Immediate 24–72 hours
  • 1Audit your Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace sign-in logs for the past 90 days for indicators of AiTM compromise: look for sessions where MFA was satisfied but the sign-in originated from unexpected IP addresses, ASNs, or geographies (Azure AD: Sign-in logs → 'Authentication Details'; Google: Admin Console → Reports → Login audit).
  • 2Revoke all active refresh tokens for any accounts showing suspicious sign-in patterns. In Azure AD: use `Revoke-AzureADUserAllRefreshToken` or the Entra admin portal. In Google Workspace: use the Admin Console to reset sign-in cookies.
  • 3Enable conditional access policies requiring compliant/managed devices for all cloud application access. In Entra ID: Security → Conditional Access → New policy → Grant: 'Require device to be marked as compliant'.
  • 4Block legacy authentication protocols (IMAP, POP3, SMTP AUTH) which bypass modern MFA controls. In Entra ID: Conditional Access → Conditions → Client apps → Block 'Exchange ActiveSync clients' and 'Other clients'.
  • 5Alert your SOC and end users about the W3LL takedown — while infrastructure has been disrupted, copycat campaigns using cloned or cached W3LL templates may persist for weeks.
● Strategic This week & beyond
  • 1Migrate all users to FIDO2/passkey-based authentication, which is cryptographically bound to the legitimate service domain and immune to AiTM proxy phishing. Prioritize high-value accounts (admins, finance, executives) and set a timeline to deprecate all non-phishing-resistant MFA methods.
  • 2Deploy a Secure Web Gateway or browser-based anti-phishing solution that performs real-time URL reputation checks and blocks known phishing proxy infrastructure patterns (e.g., newly registered domains with login portal paths).
  • 3Implement continuous session monitoring with token binding or Continuous Access Evaluation (CAE) in Microsoft 365 to detect and revoke tokens when client IP or risk conditions change mid-session.
  • 4Establish an email security posture that includes DMARC enforcement (p=reject), inbound link sandboxing, and brand impersonation detection to reduce the volume of phishing emails reaching end users.
  • 5Conduct regular tabletop exercises simulating AiTM phishing scenarios to validate detection, response, and recovery playbooks across security operations, identity management, and incident response teams.
📚Concept BriefingClick a tag to expand
Phishing-as-a-Service
A criminal business model where developers build and sell ready-made phishing toolkits — complete with templates, dashboards, and support — to other criminals on a subscription basis, much like legitimate SaaS products.
W3LL was a prime example of PhaaS, providing thousands of subscribers with turnkey phishing capabilities that drove over $20 million in fraud, illustrating how cybercrime has industrialized.
Adversary-in-the-Middle (AiTM)
An attack technique where a phishing proxy relays communication between the victim and the real service in real time, capturing session cookies that grant authenticated access — effectively bypassing traditional MFA.
AiTM session hijacking was W3LL's signature capability, rendering SMS, TOTP, and push-based MFA ineffective for victims who entered credentials on W3LL phishing pages.
Session Cookie Theft
The act of stealing the authentication token (cookie) that a web service issues after a user logs in, allowing an attacker to impersonate the user's authenticated session without needing their password or MFA code.
W3LL's AiTM proxy captured session cookies in real time, enabling attackers to hijack authenticated Microsoft 365 and Google Workspace sessions for fraud and data theft.
FIDO2 / Passkeys
A passwordless authentication standard that uses public-key cryptography tied to the legitimate service's domain, making it resistant to phishing because the credential cannot be relayed to a fake site.
FIDO2/passkeys are the primary defensive countermeasure against AiTM attacks like those powered by W3LL — organizations are urged to adopt them as traditional MFA proves insufficient.
Cybercrime Supply Chain
The ecosystem of specialized criminal service providers — toolmakers, infrastructure hosts, initial access brokers — that collectively enable large-scale cybercrime, analogous to legitimate supply chains in business.
The W3LL takedown targeted the supply chain itself (the toolmaker and platform) rather than individual phishing operators, reflecting a strategic law enforcement pivot toward high-leverage chokepoints.
🔍Threat Hunting Signals
High AiTM-indicative sign-in anomalies: MFA satisfied but session originates from unexpected infrastructure ►
Look for sign-in events where MFA was successfully completed but the resulting session's source IP, ASN, or geolocation does not match the user's known patterns. AiTM proxies relay MFA in real time, so MFA success alone does not prove legitimacy. Flag sessions where the authenticating IP differs significantly from the session's subsequent activity IP, or where sign-ins occur from hosting/VPS providers.
Azure AD Sign-in LogsGoogle Workspace Login Audit LogsCASB session logs
High Impossible travel or rapid geographic switching on authenticated sessions ►
Detect cases where the same user account authenticates from two geographically distant locations within a timeframe that makes physical travel impossible. This is a strong indicator that a session token was stolen and replayed from a different location by an attacker using harvested cookies.
Azure AD Identity ProtectionGoogle Workspace Alert CenterSIEM correlation rules
High Inbox rule or mail forwarding rule creation shortly after sign-in from new location ►
After gaining access via stolen session cookies, W3LL-enabled attackers commonly create mail forwarding rules or inbox rules to exfiltrate email or hide evidence of BEC activity. Look for new inbox rules created within minutes to hours of a sign-in flagged as risky or from a new device/location.
Microsoft 365 Unified Audit Log (Set-InboxRule, New-InboxRule)Google Workspace Admin Audit Logs (email routing changes)Exchange Online Mailbox Audit Logs
Medium Phishing page indicators: Newly registered domains with login portal URI paths ►
Monitor DNS logs and web proxy logs for user visits to recently registered domains (< 30 days old) that serve paths resembling login portals (e.g., /login, /signin, /auth, /oauth2/authorize). W3LL templates closely mimic Microsoft and Google login pages on attacker-controlled domains.
DNS query logsWeb proxy / Secure Web Gateway logsPassive DNS feeds
Medium Token replay from non-compliant or unregistered devices ►
If conditional access policies enforce device compliance, look for access attempts where a valid session token is presented from a device that is not enrolled or compliant in your MDM. This pattern suggests token theft and replay, as the attacker's machine would not be enrolled.
